Medium Priority

There's no "invisible business card" in your site's code (called Schema)

What's happening

Every website can include a block of hidden code — called schema markup — that acts like a business card for search engines and AI tools. It tells them precisely: this business is called M&I Junk Removal, it's located at [address], it's open these hours, it offers these services, it serves these cities. Your site currently has none of this hidden code. AI has to guess what your business does based on what it can read on the page — and right now, what it's reading is confusing.

Why it matters

Businesses with schema markup get recommended by AI more often. It's not magic — it just removes ambiguity. Instead of ChatGPT guessing "I think this company does junk removal in Anderson," it reads your schema and knows for certain. Certainty is what gets you recommended.

How This Actually Works

What it takes to show up when someone asks ChatGPT

You don't need to understand every technical detail. But it helps to know what AI tools are actually looking for when they decide who to recommend. It comes down to three things.

🌐

A website that works and answers real questions

No broken pages. Real service descriptions. Real pricing. Real answers in the FAQ. Location pages that are actually specific to each town.

🏷️

Invisible code that tells AI what you do

This is called schema markup. It's hidden in your site's code and tells Google and ChatGPT exactly who you are, what you do, where you operate, and when you're open — in a format the AI can read instantly without guessing.

🔗

Other trusted websites backing you up

When Yelp, Angi, Google, and local directories all say the same thing — same name, same phone, same services — AI sees that as proof your business is real and established. This is called local authority, and right now your competitors have more of it than you do.

"Think of AI like a business reference check. If someone asked five different people about M&I Junk Removal and got five different answers — two websites, conflicting service areas, roofing content mixed in — they'd probably move on. But if all five sources said the same thing and gave glowing, specific information? That's the business they'd call."
